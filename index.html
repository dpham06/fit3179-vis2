Our World Climate

Welcome to this short visualization talking about our world's climate. Our planet Earth is 4.5 billions years old which is a long time especially when you compare it to how long humans have been around, roughly 300,000 years. It is no secret that climate change is taking place at an

accelerated rate, which is largely attributed to industrialization. In the upcoming charts, we will explore the signs in our environmental data which point toward a shift in climate. Additionally, we will explore some of the consequences that accelerated climate is causing.

Average Temperature for Top 10 GDP Countries

From the chart above, it looks as if the average temperature for the listed countries has not changed for the past 100 years. You would be quite right to think that. In fact, the average global temperature has only moved by slightly more than 1 degree celsius in the last 100 years. This slight change in average temperature would be difficult to deduce from a chart.

1 degree celsius may not sound like anything to be alarmed about, throughout the course of a single day the temperature fluctuates more than that. Furthermore, 1 degree seems like even less of a worry when you find out Earth's climate naturally experiences periods of warming and cooling. To the first point, the ecosystems and life on Earth have evolved and adapted to the warming and cooling of a day and night cycle. The second point holds a bit more merit and has been used in the past by climate deniers as a counterargument to climate change. However, these periods happen over many years, allowing for slow, gradual adoption and evolution.

Figure 1: World leaders at the Paris Agreement, 2016.

1 degree of warming in such a short period is enough to warrant sounding the alarms, people and governments all over the world are calling for action. The Paris Agreement, signed by over 190 countries in 2016, seeks to limit global warming to below 2 degrees.

Figure 2: A coal power station.

The main cause for climate change is greenhouse gases (GHG). Some GHGs include carbon dioxide (CO2), methane and nitrous oxide. These gases are naturally present in our atmosphere, however their levels are kept relatively stable. GHGs cause the greenhouse gas effect, which traps heat and light that would otherwise escape our atmosphere. We depend on a certain amount of CO2 to keep our planet from freezing over. As seen from above, CO2 is being produced and polluted at an increasingly higher rate.

CO2 in particular is the GHG which humans have been polluting the most of, and is the main cause for the increase in global average temperatures. As it is the most polluted GHG, we have come focus its output over time for this chart. The streamgraph choice of idiom here illustrates the frightening increase in emissions since the early last century. World Death Rates Linked to Air Pollution (2017)

The first major consequence of our pollution of the environment is the affect it has on our air quality. As seen from above, air quality is causing deaths in countries all across the world. These deaths alone do not account for the impact reduced air quality is having on all of us as these are deaths directly linked to air quality. According to a report from the University of Chicago, air pollution is taking an average of 2.2 years off the average global life expectancy.

Our air quality suffers due to the burning of fossil fuels including

coal, oil and gas. We burn these fuels to produce energy to power our cities and transport. GHGs are one of the byproducts of these processes; however, other byproducts are also polluted into our atmosphere, further reducing air quality.

Alternative, 'green' sources of energy production which do not release any harmful byproducts are being adopted more and more. These sources include wind, solar, geothermal, hydro, nuclear and others.

Natural Disasters by Type

The second and final consequence of climate change we will explore is the number of natural disasters. In particular we are focusing on natural disasters whose rate of occurrence is affected by a change in climate, for example we are not including earthquakes. We can see across the board an increase in the number of natural disasters.

Natural disasters have multitudes of consequences which can be summed up by the number of human lives lost and economic damage sustained. As the climate worsens the rate at which these disasters

happen will continue to increase. Food shortages are an especially worrying consequence that can happen as a result of natural disasters which we have been witnessing in recent history. A number of food shortages at the same time could lead to mass starvation.

The future is not all bleak. The world has woken up to accept that we are the cause of such events. Initiatives all over the world are beginning to tackle our emissions targets.
